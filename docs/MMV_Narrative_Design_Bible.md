# 1. What This Game Is

MMV puts a player inside a single human life and asks them to live it — not as a hero, not as a character archetype, but as a person navigating competing demands with limited time, energy, money, and will.

The player will always have more choices available than they can take. Choosing is not completing a menu — it is closing doors. Every decision leaves something behind, and the shape of what gets left behind is the story.

## Core Statement

| Resource | Role |
|---|---|
| Time | Hard limit. Three slots per day. Something always slips. |
| Energy | Lived capacity. What you can do is not always what you have energy for. |
| Money | Opportunity gate. Some doors only open if you can afford them. |
| Skill | Capability constraint. Preparation changes outcomes. |
| Identity | Pattern of protection. Your choices reveal what you guard. |
| Relationships | Consequence amplifier. Others remember what you do. |

## What Makes This Different

- **There is no optimal path.** Every playthrough creates a different life, not a better or worse score.
- **Preclusion is the mechanic.** Choosing X closes Y permanently. The player never sees everything in one run.
- **Character emerges from accumulation.** No single choice defines you. Patterns across choices do.
- **Reflection is the payoff.** At arc end, the game mirrors back what you prioritised and what it cost.
- **Replay is structured reinvention.** Players return knowing what they missed and try a different life.

## What This Game Is Not

Do not confuse MMV with adjacent genres. It is not:

- A choice game where all content eventually becomes accessible through enough playthroughs
- A stat-building game where players optimise toward a target character build
- A morality system where choices are scored as good or bad
- A party college fantasy or a campus romance sim
- A dark academia mystery with secret societies and forbidden knowledge

The character archetypes document (Jock, Nerd, Mean Girl, Struggling Scholar) exists as a **negative reference** — a map of what lazy college narrative looks like. Every scene in MMV should earn its specificity by refusing those defaults.

# 2. The Preclusion System

Preclusion is the foundational mechanic of MMV. It is what separates this from a choice game where all content eventually becomes accessible. In MMV, content is permanently unavailable in a given run if a different path was taken.

Every meaningful slot-level choice must close at least one named door. The player will not always know which door they have closed until it matters.

## Anatomy of a Preclusion Chain

A preclusion chain has four parts:

1. **The visible choice** — what the player sees and selects
2. **The hidden door closed** — what becomes unavailable, often unknown at time of choice
3. **The downstream consequence** — when and how the closure is felt
4. **The character tag fired** — what the choice says about who the player is becoming

> **Rule:** The player only sees Part 1 in the moment. Parts 2 and 3 arrive later — sometimes much later. Part 4 is never shown directly. It accumulates silently.

## The Five Preclusion Design Rules

**Rule 1 — Every slot must have at least one permanent close.**
Not a delay. A close. The job table doesn't come back. A person's first morning in a place doesn't replay.

**Rule 2 — At least one path should open something unexpected.**
The player who chose "reflection over practical action" should be rewarded eventually — but not immediately. This models the way good decisions often don't pay off on the timeline you expected.

**Rule 3 — The player should not fully understand what they missed until it matters.**
The bookstore player doesn't know about the job table until they feel money tightness two weeks later. Revelation should feel like memory, not notification.

**Rule 4 — Character tags fire from the choice logic, not the outcome.**
You get Practical Hustle from going to the job table regardless of whether the money situation improves. The choice itself is the character statement.

**Rule 5 — Downstream consequences must feel like life, not punishment.**
Eating alone on Day 2 isn't a penalty notification. It's just what the dining hall looks like when you haven't built anything yet.

## Worked Example — Day 1, Morning Slot (Arc One: College)

The player wakes up. The roommate mentions the campus job table runs 9–11am only, today only. A flyer is on the floor near the door. Money band: Tight.

At the same moment:
- A note on the dorm whiteboard: Carol called from home last night. No number left. She'll try again.
- Down the hall, Marcus asks if you know where the bookstore is. He seems worth knowing.
- The class schedule shows the academic open house for undeclared majors at 10am.

Three pulls. One morning slot. The player must pick one.

---

### Chain A — Go to the Job Table

| Element | Content |
|---|---|
| Visible choice | Get there early, wait in a short line, land a dining hall shift starting next week. |
| Hidden door closed | Marcus went to the bookstore alone. Met two people in line. That cluster becomes the Week 3 study group. You can still join, but as a latecomer. |
| Downstream consequence | Work schedule begins. Tuesday and Thursday 6–8am shifts conflict with an organic early study session that forms later. You feel this constraint before you understand it. |
| Character tag fired | Practical Hustle +1. Money band: Tight → Okay (slow). Social Ease: missed opportunity. |

---

### Chain B — Follow Marcus to the Bookstore

| Element | Content |
|---|---|
| Visible choice | Go with him. Long line. You talk. He's from Ohio, pre-law, nervous but performing confidence. Meet Diane — sharp and slightly guarded. |
| Hidden door closed | Job table closes at 11am. You're still in line at 11:05. Money band stays Tight. |
| Downstream consequence | Two weeks later, an opportunity costs money you don't have. You remember this morning without knowing you could have changed it. |
| Character tag fired | Social Ease +1. Practical Hustle: missed. Diane clocked your hesitation before joining the line — reliability: neutral, trust: slightly curious. |

---

### Chain C — Go to the Academic Open House

| Element | Content |
|---|---|
| Visible choice | Sit in a wood-panelled room. Dr. Okafor talks about the difference between choosing a major and choosing a direction. You speak once. He hears you. |
| Hidden door closed | Both Marcus and the job table. Marcus becomes a familiar face with no context. Money band stays Tight. |
| Downstream consequence | Dr. Okafor recognises you by name in the hall on Day 3. That's rare. A thread opens — not immediately useful, but present across the arc. |
| Character tag fired | Study Discipline +1. Risk +1 (chose reflection over practical or social). Social Ease: missed. Money band: unchanged. |

---

### What Three Weeks Later Looks Like

| Choice made | Marcus cluster | Money band | Dr. Okafor thread | Work constraint |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Job table | Latecomer — can join | Okay | Closed | Yes: Tue/Thu 6–8am |
| Bookstore | Insider from Day 1 | Tight | Closed | No |
| Open house | Outsider | Tight | Open | No |

No path is optimal. Each creates a different version of the player's life with different pressures bearing down in Week 3. The player made one choice at 9am on Day 1 and they're still living in it.

# 3. The Three-Layer System

Every choice in MMV operates across three interacting layers. Arc One activates a minimal version of each. Later arcs deepen all three without re-architecting the system.

## Layer 1 — Structural Scarcity (External Constraints)

These are the finite resources that determine what is possible.

### Time — Primary Constraint

Three daily slots: Morning, Afternoon, Evening. Some activities cost two slots. Time scarcity guarantees something slips every day. This is non-negotiable by design — if nothing slips, the pressure isn't real.

### Energy — Lived Capacity

| State | Conditions | Effect on play |
|---|---|---|
| High | Well-rested, low conflict, recent success | Full access to confrontation options; skill modifiers fully active |
| Moderate | Normal day, moderate demand | Baseline — most options available |
| Low | Overcommitted, stacked conflict, skipped recovery | Avoidance options become more attractive; confrontation harder; skill less effective |

Energy regenerates with: rest, social ease (sometimes), success events.

Low energy is not a punishment mechanic. It is the system modelling how life pushes people toward choices they wouldn't endorse when they're running on empty — and that is precisely the point.

### Money — Opportunity Band

| Band | Effect |
|---|---|
| Tight | Some choices unavailable. Some require extra time (job shift instead of attending event). Stress indicator rises. |
| Okay | Standard access. No friction removal, no hard blocks. |
| Comfortable | Some friction removed. Certain social opportunities become available. |

Money changes slowly. Never display exact numbers in Arc One. Express it through friction events and blocked choices, not a visible meter.

### Skill — Capability Flags

Arc One uses four low-resolution flags. Skill grows by repetition, not by spending points.

| Flag | Grows when | Modifies |
|---|---|---|
| Study Discipline | Choosing academic over social/practical repeatedly | Outcome quality on academic challenges; energy cost of study events |
| Social Ease | Choosing connection; initiating with strangers; showing up | Success probability in relationship bids; energy drain from social events |
| Assertiveness | Confronting instead of deferring; setting terms | Confrontation outcomes; NPC trust response |
| Practical Hustle | Choosing work, logistics, self-sufficiency | Money band trajectory; access to practical opportunity threads |

## Layer 2 — Psychological Tradeoffs (Internal Patterns)

These three axes track the shape of a player's decision-making. They are never displayed directly. They feed the Reflection Engine at arc end.

| Axis | Description | Reflection it generates |
|---|---|---|
| Risk ↔ Safety | Are you choosing exposure or protection? | What you protected vs. what you let slip |
| People ↔ Achievement | Are you choosing connection or accomplishment? | Who you showed up for vs. what you built |
| Confront ↔ Avoid | Are you meeting tension or deferring it? | What tensions you let compound |

Every tagged choice increments one or more of these counters. Patterns across many choices produce the reflection — not single moments.

In later arcs, energy patterns, money patterns, and skill patterns are added to this tracking:
- *"You repeatedly ran yourself thin."*
- *"You avoided financial risk."*
- *"You relied on charm over preparation."*

## Layer 3 — Relational Consequences (Social Memory)

NPCs remember what the player does. Not as a reputation score, but as a private state that changes how they behave.

```
npcMemory = {
  reliability:    number,   // Did you show up when expected?
  trust:          number,   // Do they believe what you say and do?
  emotional_load: number    // How much have they given vs. received?
}
```

| Player action | NPC memory effect |
|---|---|
| Repeated deferral | reliability decreases — future invitations become conditional |
| Direct confrontation | trust may increase OR decrease depending on context and prior reliability |
| High-energy generosity | emotional_load shifts — NPC becomes willing to extend themselves |
| Showing up consistently | reliability builds — NPC includes you in things you didn't ask for |

Arc One reflection may include one relational line: *"Others experienced you as steady but distant."* Later arcs deepen this into trust loss, loyalty conflicts, and attachment patterns.

## The Choice Schema

Every meaningful choice carries this data structure:

```
choice = {
  time_cost:          1 or 2,
  energy_cost:        low / moderate / high,
  money_effect:       optional (band shift or gate),
  skill_requirement:  optional flag,
  skill_modifier:     optional flag increment,
  identity_tags:      [risk, people, confront, etc.],
  relational_effect:  [npc_id: {reliability, trust, emotional_load}],
  preclusion:         [thread_ids or npc_ids permanently closed]
}
```

Every meaningful choice must populate at least `time_cost`, `identity_tags`, and `preclusion`.

# 4. The Pillar Bible

Pillars are the fixed thematic questions of a human life. They do not change between arcs. The pressure applied to them does.

The design problem: life can go a thousand ways, but story needs a few stable shapes. The solution is **infinite routes, finite questions**.

## The Six Pillars

| Pillar | Tension | College arc pressure |
|---|---|---|
| Belonging | Earned vs. Performed | Primary — who accepts you, and at what cost to self? |
| Courage | Truth vs. Approval | Primary — do you risk rejection to be seen? |
| Agency | Act vs. Drift | Secondary — do you initiate or wait? |
| Integrity | Values vs. Advantage | Secondary — emerging, not yet dominant |
| Craft | Devotion vs. Validation | Light — seeds only, matures in later arcs |
| Love | Attach vs. Avoid | Light — seeds only |

## Three Modes Per Pillar

Do not write dozens of variants per pillar. Write three portrayals. Combinations of modes across pillars create perceived variety.

| Mode | Description |
|---|---|
| Mode A | One coherent life-stance — initiated, owned, expressed |
| Mode B | An alternate coherent life-stance — responsive, adapted, curated |
| Shadow mode | The coping strategy / failure pattern — avoided, rationalised, deferred |

### Example — Belonging
- **Earned (Mode A):** Shows up awkward, stays honest, accepts discomfort as the cost of real connection
- **Performed (Mode B):** Shows up masked, curates presentation, achieves inclusion at the cost of authenticity
- **Shadow (Drifter):** Avoids the bid entirely. Tells themselves they didn't really want it.

### Example — Agency
- **Actor (Mode A):** Initiates, risks, owns outcomes
- **Adapter (Mode B):** Responds well, but rarely initiates
- **Drifter (Shadow):** Avoids choice, lets others decide, rationalises it as flexibility

## Crystallising Moments

Each pillar needs 3–5 crystallisers per arc. A crystalliser is a high-stakes scene that forces the pillar tension into the open. The same crystalliser core can be skinned across different surface contexts.

| Pillar | Crystalliser question | Arc One skins |
|---|---|---|
| Belonging | Will you pay the social cost of honesty? | Floor meeting confession; study group disagreement; roommate confrontation |
| Courage | Do you risk rejection to be seen? | Introducing yourself unprompted; voicing an unpopular opinion; telling someone what you actually think |
| Agency | Do you initiate or wait to be chosen? | Joining a group vs. waiting for an invitation; declaring direction vs. following others |
| Integrity | Will you take the advantage if the cost falls on someone else? | Borrowing notes without credit; taking the better assignment slot at someone's expense |

**Why this works:** you reuse the same moral question across many surface contexts. Players encounter it multiple times in different forms without feeling repetitive, because the skin changes even when the question doesn't.

## The Thread Tag Card

Every thread must be tagged before writing begins:

```
Thread: [name]
Primary pillar:   [pillar]
Secondary pillar: [pillar]
Question asked:   [one line — the decision the player is being asked about themselves]
Mode A answer:    [what Mode A looks like in this thread]
Mode B answer:    [what Mode B looks like in this thread]
Shadow answer:    [what avoidance looks like in this thread]
Precludes:        [thread IDs or NPC relationships this closes]
Gates on:         [what must be true for this thread to be available]
```

### Example Tag Card

```
Thread: "The Invite"
Primary pillar:   Belonging
Secondary pillar: Courage
Question asked:   "Do you risk rejection to be seen?"
Mode A answer:    Go. Be awkward. Stay honest.
Mode B answer:    Go. Mask. Curate your presentation carefully.
Shadow answer:    Don't go. Rationalise it as not being your scene.
Precludes:        [floor_social_cluster_thread] if shadow chosen
Gates on:         Day 1 evening slot, roommate reliability >= neutral
```

## Preventing Optimal Play

The most dangerous failure mode: pillars become build paths. Players optimise toward one pillar and feel no cost.

Prevent this with:
- **Non-linear payoffs** — sometimes the right move hurts short-term
- **Social memory** — NPCs respond to patterns, not single choices
- **Tradeoffs** — every pillar has upsides and costs
- **Contradiction** — a player can be high Craft and low Love; high Belonging but performed. This is allowed and correct. Life is messy.

## The Balance Test

After any run, the player should be able to answer three questions:

1. **Who did I become?** (pillars)
2. **What did it cost?** (tradeoffs)
3. **What would I try next time?** (forks)

If yes: the run was meaningful. If not: a pillar was not under enough pressure.

# 5. The Thread Deck

Threads are the unit of authored content. They are not scenes — they are structured story problems that play out across multiple scenes.

## Thread Skeleton

Every thread is built from this fixed skeleton. This ensures any thread fits the system regardless of topic.

| Beat | Function | Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| Hook | Establishes the want. What the player is drawn toward. | 1 scene. Should feel like an opportunity, not a trap. |
| Escalation | The cost appears. Something about this is harder or more expensive than it looked. | 1–2 scenes. The player should feel the pull before they feel the cost. |
| Choice | A tradeoff. Two or more pulls that cannot both be satisfied. | 1 scene. This is the slot-level choice. It must fire preclusion. |
| Consequence | Social or internal result. Not immediate judgment — just what happens. | 1–2 scenes, possibly delayed by days. |
| Echo | A quiet beat or reflection tag. The thread's meaning settles. | Often embedded in arc-end reflection rather than a discrete scene. |

## Gate Types

Gates ensure not everything can happen in one life. They give a life its shape.

| Gate type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Identity gate | Requires a pillar mode or threshold | "Belonging: performed" opens different scenes than "earned" |
| Commitment gate | You can only join one of several communities at a time | Band vs. debate vs. athletics — picking one forecloses the others |
| Time/energy gate | Pursuing X reliably means sacrificing Y | Working job shifts means missing the evening social window |
| Relationship gate | Intimacy requires repeated bids over time | Some NPC threads only open after reliability > 2 |

## Thread Families

Build threads in families of 4–8 that explore the same core question. Players will experience different instances of the same theme across a run, which feels like life.

### Example Family — "Approval vs. Truth"

All five threads ask the same question in different contexts. A player who plays one will not necessarily encounter the others in the same run.

1. Friend group pressure
2. Romantic honesty
3. Career ambition vs. stated values
4. Family expectations
5. Public embarrassment

## Scale for Arc One

| Item | Target |
|---|---|
| Total threads | 30–50 |
| Beats per thread | 5 (skeleton) |
| Variants per thread | 2–3 (via gating/skins) |
| Crystallising moments per pillar | 3–5 |

Combinations do the heavy lifting. 40 threads with 2–3 variants each, across 6 pillars, produces a perceived variety far greater than the authored count suggests.

# 6. How to Build a Thread (Content Creation Guide)

This section is the working guide for writers building content. Follow these steps for every thread.

## Step 1 — Assign the Tag Card

Fill out the tag card before writing any prose. If you cannot complete the tag card, the thread is not ready to write.

Required fields:
- Primary and secondary pillar
- The single question the thread asks about the player
- The three mode answers
- What it precludes
- What gates it

## Step 2 — Map the Preclusion Chain

Before writing prose, map the chain explicitly:

```
Slot: [morning / afternoon / evening, Day X]
This choice closes: [name what is permanently unavailable]
This choice opens (delayed): [name what becomes available later — or "nothing visible yet"]
Character tags fired: [list skill flags and identity axis increments]
NPC memory effects: [list affected NPCs and how their state changes]
Downstream scene: [name the scene where closure is felt, and approximately when]
```

Do not begin writing until this map exists. If the preclusion chain is unclear, the thread will not produce meaningful pressure.

## Step 3 — Write the Hook Scene

The hook should feel like an opportunity, not a trap. The player should want it. If the player doesn't want it, there is no pressure in choosing something else over it.

Hook scene checklist:
- [ ] Is there something genuinely appealing here?
- [ ] Is the setting grounded in physical 1983 reality?
- [ ] Does at least one other pull exist simultaneously?
- [ ] Is the time slot cost legible without stating it?

## Step 4 — Write the Escalation

The cost appears. This is not when the player chooses — it is when they see that choosing will cost something. The escalation should raise the stakes before the choice moment, not reveal them after.

## Step 5 — Write the Choice Node

This is the slot-level decision. Rules:

- Present 2–4 options. Never more than 4.
- Each option must be genuinely plausible. No decoys.
- Each option must fire different preclusion.
- Do not editorialize in the choice text. State what each option is, not what it means.
- The player should feel the weight of closing a door, even if they don't know which door they're closing.

## Step 6 — Write the Consequence Scenes

Consequences arrive at two speeds:

**Immediate** (same day or next day): the texture of the choice lands. The player sees what they got and senses — vaguely — what they didn't.

**Delayed** (days or weeks later): the closed door becomes visible. This should land as recognition, not punishment. *"I remember that morning."*

## Step 7 — Write the Echo

The echo is the quiet close of the thread. It is often a single line of narration or a NPC moment that doesn't demand attention. Its job is to plant the reflection tag that the Reflection Engine will surface at arc end.

Examples:
- *"You still haven't called Carol back."*
- *"Marcus waves at you across the quad. He's with his study group."*
- *"Dr. Okafor's office light is on when you pass it at 9pm."*

These lines do not explain anything. They just exist — and at arc end, they will mean something.

## Content Writing Rules

**Do not name the pillar in the prose.** The player should never see "Belonging" or "Courage" as labels. The question is asked through what happens, not what it's called.

**Do not score the choice.** No line of narration should indicate that a choice was better or worse. Show what happened. Let the player decide what it means.

**Do not repeat the same escalation pattern across threads.** If three threads in a row escalate by revealing a financial cost, the system becomes predictable. Vary the nature of the cost — time, energy, relationship, self-image.

**Déjà vu must appear at least twice in Arc One, subtly.** Not as a plot mechanic — as a texture. A line that feels already spoken. A corner remembered before the map is checked. A joke predicted before it lands. Small. Quiet. Psychological.

**Physical anchors are mandatory.** Every scene must include at least one grounding detail from the 1983 setting. The dorm hallway smells of industrial cleaner. Someone is blasting The Police. The class schedule is folded in a back pocket. If it doesn't feel physically real, it won't feel emotionally real.

# 7. Setting & Era

## The Lock

**Year: 1983.** Not retro cosplay. Not parody. It should feel ordinary, textured, and slightly overwhelming.

**Location:** A midsized American college. 8,000–12,000 students. Brick buildings. One central quad. 1970s dorm architecture. A mix of local and out-of-state students.

This scale matters because it determines:
- How overwhelming arrival feels
- How anonymous a person can be
- How easy it is to disappear

## What Exists and What Doesn't

**Does not exist:**
- Smartphones
- Personal computers in dorm rooms
- Email
- Texting

**Does exist:**
- Bulletin boards with hand-drawn flyers
- Printed schedules
- Dorm floor phones (shared, public)
- Cassette players
- Paper maps
- Bookstore lines with cardboard boxes of textbooks
- Physical checks for tuition and books
- Orientation events with paper flyers
- Mix tapes made from radio (waiting for the DJ not to talk over the intro)
- Record stores as social hubs
- Arcade cabinets in the student union

## Why 1983 Matters for Pressure Design

The absence of digital tools is not nostalgia — it is a pressure mechanic. In 1983:
- You cannot look up where the bookstore is. You have to ask or guess.
- You cannot text someone you just met. You have to knock on their door or leave a note on the whiteboard.
- Information asymmetry is real. Not knowing something costs time.
- Decisions made without full information are the norm, not the exception.

This physical friction amplifies the time scarcity system. Every act of navigation, coordination, and communication costs a real slice of a slot.

## Cultural Texture — What Was in the Air

**Geopolitical mood:** Cold War anxiety in the background. Reagan optimism on the surface. The Day After (1983 nuclear war TV movie) deeply affected young viewers. Nuclear freeze movements were active on campuses. Students were aware the world was unstable — but also that money and ambition were suddenly fashionable again.

**Music as identity signalling:** A record collection was social currency. What someone was playing said something about who they were.

- Mainstream: The Police, Bruce Springsteen, Michael Jackson, Billy Joel
- New Wave / Alternative: Talking Heads, The Cure, Duran Duran, R.E.M.
- Metal: Van Halen, AC/DC
- Early hip-hop: Grandmaster Flash (emerging, not yet dominant)

**Economic context:** Early 80s recession transitioning to Wall Street optimism. High interest rates. Rising yuppie culture. Some students rejected consumerism. Others leaned into upward mobility unapologetically. This tension — meaning vs. money — is available as an early Integrity/Craft pillar seed.

**Parental relationship:** Less open emotional dialogue than today. Therapy culture was not mainstream on campuses. Parents had lived through Vietnam and Watergate — many students saw them as compromised or disillusioned, while remaining financially dependent on them. Cultural gap: parents = Beatles, Woodstock, protest. Students = post-idealism, early cynicism.

## Mandatory Physical Anchors

Writers must use these grounding details. If a scene feels unrooted, reach for this list:

- Dorm hallway smells faintly of industrial cleaner
- Doors propped open with sneakers
- Someone blasting The Police or Talking Heads
- Hand-written "Welcome Class of '87" banner
- Long line at bookstore with cardboard boxes of textbooks
- Cafeteria trays and plastic cups
- Paper class schedules folded in a back pocket
- Phone messages written on a whiteboard near the dorm phone

## Slang — Use Lightly

These would be recognisable in 1983. Salt, don't saturate. Overusing slang reads as parody.

- Everyday: Awesome, Rad, Totally, Bummer, Bogus, Gnarly, Dude (becoming gender-neutral), Chill out, No way
- Subculture labels: Preppy, Punk, New Waver, Yuppie (used cynically), Sellout
- Valley Girl wave (post-1982): For sure, Grody — use sparingly

## The Emotional Frame

Three overlapping feelings at the start of Arc One:

**Overwhelm:** Too many doors. Too many names. Too many maps.

**Opportunity:** You could be anyone here. No one knows you.

**Déjà vu:** Something about this feels slightly familiar. Not supernatural. Not horror. Just... familiar.

## Déjà Vu — How It Appears

Not:
- Flashy glitch
- Overt time-travel exposition

Instead:
- A line feels already spoken
- A hallway corner feels remembered before you turn it
- You predict a joke before it lands
- A face feels known before introduction

> *As Miguel laughs, you know he's about to slap the table before he does.*
>
> *You turn left toward the bookstore before checking the map — and you're right.*
>
> *The floor meeting script sounds rehearsed, like you've heard it before.*

Déjà vu must appear at least twice in Arc One. It should create a moment of quiet disorientation — not enough to explain, just enough to feel. On replay, the player will understand it differently.

# 8. The Reflection Engine

The Reflection Engine runs at the end of each arc. It reads the accumulated state across all three layers and produces a mirror: what the player prioritised, what it cost, how they moved under pressure.

## What the Engine Reads

- Identity axis skew (Risk/Safety, People/Achievement, Confront/Avoid counts)
- Expired and closed opportunities (what the player never took)
- Energy usage patterns (did they regularly run thin? protect themselves?)
- Money band trajectory (did it improve? worsen? stay stuck?)
- Skill flag accumulation (what grew and what stayed flat)
- NPC relational states (reliability, trust, emotional_load per NPC)

## What the Engine Outputs

The reflection is not a score. It is a portrait. It should feel like something a perceptive friend might say — accurate, a little uncomfortable, earned.

### Arc One Reflection (minimal version)

- What you prioritised (identity pattern)
- What slipped (time/opportunity)
- How you carried yourself under pressure (energy pattern)
- One relational observation (if a NPC pattern is strong enough)

### Mid-Semester Reflection (added)

- A money tradeoff line
- A skill tradeoff line
- A trust impact line

### Chapter End Reflection (full version)

- Narrative drift: *"You became someone who..."*
- Scar & Strength tags: what this chapter cost and what it built
- What you protected most
- Rare: a regret trigger (only when a specific closed door had major downstream consequence)

## Reflection Tone Rules

The reflection speaks in second person, past tense, with specificity. It does not moralize. It does not praise or condemn. It observes.

**Do:** *"You consistently chose the practical path when the social one felt uncertain. The money pressure eased. The cluster you didn't enter formed without you."*

**Don't:** *"You made some great choices and some poor choices. Your practicality is a real strength but don't forget to connect with others!"*

The reflection should land as recognition — the player should feel that the game saw what they actually did, not what they intended to do.

# 9. The Replay Loop

The replay loop is what transforms a single run into a meaningful life simulation. Players complete a run, receive a reflection, and are offered a replay intention. The intention subtly reshapes the next run without railroading it.

## Replay Intention Options (Arc One)

| Intention | What it biases |
|---|---|
| Take more visible risks | Dialogue options that risk social exposure appear more prominently; safety options are slightly less visible |
| Protect my focus | Academic and practical threads weighted upward; social invitations arrive later |
| Address tension early | Confrontation options presented first; avoidance options require an extra beat |
| Lean into connection | Relational threads weighted upward; NPC invitations more frequent |

## What Replay Intention Does Not Do

- It does not unlock content unavailable to a non-biased run
- It does not guarantee a different outcome
- It does not make previously-closed doors reopen unless the player makes different choices

Replay intention is subtle pressure, not a route switch.

## The Replay Motivation

The player's motivation to replay comes from three sources:

1. **The closed door they saw.** The reflection named something that was available and missed. They want to try the other path.
2. **The character they became.** They want to see who they are if they make different foundational choices.
3. **Déjà vu.** On a second or third run, moments that were subtly familiar on the first run now carry weight. The player is not replaying a story — they are reliving a life with more knowledge than a person normally has.

# 10. Chapter Architecture

MMV is structured as chapters across a human life. Each chapter tests different pillars under different pressures. The system architecture does not change — only what the world demands of the player.

## The Five Chapters

| Chapter | Life stage | Primary pillars tested | What the world demands |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — College | Identity & Experimentation | Belonging, Courage | Find out who you are in a room full of strangers |
| 2 — Early Adulthood (~22–30) | Agency & Exposure | Agency, Craft | Reality tests your self-story: money, work, loneliness |
| 3 — Commitments (~30–40) | Binding Choices | Integrity, Love | Tradeoffs become non-negotiable |
| 4 — Maintenance & Meaning (~40–50) | Strain, Care, Recalibration | Care, Meaning | Responsibilities peak; identity under sustained pressure |
| 5 — Now (55+) | Integration & Reframing | Integration | "Am I trapped, or can I choose?" |

## Chapter Boundaries

Chapter breaks are triggered by **life state transitions**, not ages. Ages are approximate labels for orientation. The mechanics trigger on:

- Leaving school / first full-time work
- Moving cities / first stable home
- Long-term partnership / first child / caregiving begins
- Career plateau / health signals / children becoming independent

## How Pillars Evolve Across Chapters

The same pillar question shows up differently at each life stage. This is elegant reuse — the player is not encountering a new question, they are encountering the same question in a context that changes everything about the answer.

| Pillar | College | Early Adulthood | Commitments | Maintenance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Belonging | Who accepts you? | Who stayed? | Who still knows you? | Who can you rely on? |
| Integrity | Will you take the advantage? | Will you keep the values that cost you? | Will you compromise what you said you were? | What did you protect? |
| Love | Do you let someone in? | Do you stay or leave? | Did you attach to the right things? | Did you tend what mattered? |

## Chapter Five — The "Now" Frame

Chapter Five functions as both a bookend and a repeatable return point. It is where the game's core message lands: you are not trapped. Every chapter is available for reinvestment.

Structure:
- Cold open: the player at 55+, in a moment of reflection
- Middle: replay reveals patterns and surfaces agency
- End: choose a new direction now, or choose another chapter to replay with intention

This is the architecture that makes the game feel like a life rather than a sequence of stories.

# 11. Arc One Deep Dive — College: First Week

## Setting Lock

Year: 1983. Day 1 of college. The player woke up this morning either alone or with a roommate they barely know (Option A: roommate arrived yesterday, recommended for Arc One clarity and relational testing).

## Structural Rules for Arc One Content

Before writing any scene, confirm it adheres to these rules:

1. Player must choose between social and practical tasks on Day 1
2. At least one opportunity expires on Day 1 with no recovery path
3. Roommate interaction must happen Day 1
4. Day 2 must show a consequence from a Day 1 choice
5. Déjà vu must appear at least twice, subtly
6. Every morning, afternoon, and evening slot must have more options than can be taken

## The Day 1 Event Map

### Morning Slot — Options

| Option | Time cost | Precludes | Opens |
|---|---|---|---|
| Campus job table (9–11am only, today only) | 1 slot | Marcus bookstore connection | Dining hall job thread; Practical Hustle flag |
| Bookstore with Marcus | 1 slot | Job table permanently | Marcus cluster; Diane relationship thread |
| Academic open house (undeclared) | 1 slot | Both Marcus and job table | Dr. Okafor thread (delayed payoff) |

### Afternoon Slot — Options

| Option | Time cost | Precludes | Opens |
|---|---|---|---|
| Orientation fair on the quad | 1 slot | Quiet solo campus exploration | 2–3 NPC first meetings; club/activity awareness |
| Solo campus navigation (find key buildings) | 1 slot | Fair connections | Spatial confidence (reduces friction cost on Day 2) |
| Return to dorm — unpack, reset | 1 slot | Both of above | Energy recovery; roommate extended conversation thread |

### Evening Slot — Options

| Option | Time cost | Precludes | Opens |
|---|---|---|---|
| Floor meeting (mandatory texture, but depth varies) | 1 slot | Freshman social event | Floor relationship foundation; 2–4 floor NPC introductions |
| Freshman social event | 1 slot | Floor meeting depth (attend briefly, then leave = both, but shallowly) | Wider campus social web; potential romantic thread seed |
| Study / prepare for Day 2 | 1 slot | Both social options | Study Discipline flag; energy preserved for Day 2 |

## The Roommate

One roommate, already present (arrived yesterday). Recommended NPC for Arc One relational testing because:
- Provides immediate, unavoidable relational tension
- NPC memory is easy to track (shared living space = daily interaction)
- Relational consequences are legible without campus-wide reputation mechanics

The roommate interaction must happen on Day 1. The depth and quality of that interaction is determined by afternoon slot choice. Player who returns to dorm in the afternoon gets an extended version. Player who attends both fair and social gets a brief, functional version.

## Player State at Start

**Emotionally:**
- Slightly untethered
- Trying to appear composed
- Afraid of looking foolish
- Curious
- Aware this matters

**Psychologically:**
- *"I could reinvent myself."*
- *"Don't mess this up."*
- *"Why does this feel familiar?"*

## What Day 2 Must Show

Day 2 is the first consequence day. It must make at least one Day 1 choice visible as a consequence. Specifically:

- If job table chosen: work schedule appears with its first slot conflict
- If bookstore/Marcus chosen: Marcus nods across the dining hall — cluster visible, player's relationship to it established
- If open house chosen: Dr. Okafor recognition moment in the hall
- If floor meeting attended: at least one floor NPC initiates contact
- If social event attended: at least one contact attempts follow-up
- If player studied: Day 2 first class has a slightly easier texture — player notices they were the only one who read the syllabus

## Arc One Visible Systems

| System | Visible to player? | How it presents |
|---|---|---|
| Time slots | Yes | 3 slots clearly available; choice closes the slot |
| Energy | Yes (simple) | High / Moderate / Low indicator |
| Stress | Yes | Colour indicator — no numbers |
| Money band | No — contextual | Expressed through friction and blocked choices only |
| Skill flags | No — hidden | Never displayed; felt through outcome quality |
| Identity axes | No — hidden | Feed reflection only |
| NPC memory | No — hidden | Expressed through NPC dialogue and behaviour |

# 12. Writing Rules

These rules apply to every piece of authored content in MMV. They are not style preferences — they are structural requirements.

## The Non-Negotiables

**Never name the pillar in the prose.** The player must never see "Belonging" or "Courage" as labels in the narrative. The question is asked through what happens, not what it's called.

**Never score the choice.** No line of narration indicates a choice was better or worse. Show what happened. Let the player decide what it means.

**Never deliver punishment as notification.** A consequence is not a game message. It is the next thing that happens.

**Never write a decoy choice.** Every option presented to the player must be genuinely plausible. A choice the player would never make is not a choice — it is friction.

**Never repeat the same escalation pattern across adjacent threads.** Vary the nature of the cost. If the last thread escalated via financial pressure, the next should escalate via relational or self-image pressure.

## Tone

The tone leans grounded and understated — a realistic campus novel tone, not a heightened literary one. Interiority is present but restrained. The déjà vu element provides the only poetic licence, and even that is quiet.

The setting should feel ordinary and slightly overwhelming. Not retro. Not ironic. Real.

## Voice

The narration is second person, present tense. The player is "you." Events are described as they happen.

Second person creates identification without explaining the player's feelings. Show the situation. Trust the player to feel it.

## Specificity Over Generality

Every scene needs a specific physical detail. Every NPC needs at least one specific trait that is not their role. Every choice needs a specific consequence that lands at a specific later moment.

Generic writing produces generic pressure. Specific writing produces the feeling that this particular life, on this particular morning, in this particular dorm, mattered.

## What Good Looks Like

A well-written MMV thread is one where, at arc end, the player can answer:

- *"I remember when I chose that. I remember what I thought it cost."*
- *"I didn't know that door was there until it wasn't."*
- *"That's what I'm trying next time."*

If those three things are true, the thread did its job.
