## Day 3, Morning — Beat 2A: "The Hallway Comment"
*Storylet: `hallway_morning_day3` — SHIPPED 2026-04-25*

### Preamble
The hallway is busy enough at this hour that you don't have to walk alone. Doug nods as you come out of your room. Keith catches up from the other direction, mid-conversation, and just folds you into it.

### Node — passing Doug
Doug raises his chin in the corridor. He's got a backpack slung over one shoulder, hair still wet. Keith comes up behind him talking about a movie he saw over the summer.

**Micro-choices:**
- "Hey, Doug."
- Just nod. Keep walking with them.

### Node — Keith's comment
Keith is in the middle of a story. "—and the lead actor, you know that guy, he was in that thing — anyway, there's this scene where he just *cries*, like, full-on. For like a minute." Doug snorts. Keith laughs: "Yeah. That was so gay."

Doug laughs. The conversation keeps moving. Mike's door is open at the end of the hall — he's at his desk, half-turned, papers in his lap. He doesn't look up.

You're standing right there.

**Micro-choices:**
- Say nothing. It's not worth it.
- Make a face. Change the subject.
- "That word's kind of…" — trail off before finishing.

### Node — friction response (variant: absorbed)
Keith finishes the story. Doug puts in a punchline. The three of you keep walking.

### Node — friction response (variant: deflected)
You change the subject — something about whether the dining hall is open this early. Keith blinks at the swerve, picks up the new thread. Doug glances at you for a second and then doesn't.

### Node — friction response (variant: challenged)
The sentence comes out before you've decided what the rest of it is. Keith goes quiet for a second — not angry, just thrown. Doug fills the silence with something else, something about the dining hall. The hallway keeps moving around you.

Mike, at his desk, has stopped writing.

### Terminal choices
- Head to class with them.
- Peel off. You forgot something in your room.

## Day 4, Morning — Beat 2C: "The Quad Walk"
*Storylet: `walk_to_class_day4` — NEW*

### Preamble
Doug catches you in the hall. He and Keith are walking to Western Civ; same building as your morning class. You fall in.

### Node — leaving the dorm
Outside is brighter than you expected. The grass on the quad is still wet — sprinklers ran early. A handful of students cutting across, paper schedules tucked under arms. Keith is talking about his weekend.

### Node — women pass
A group of three women — sophomores, probably — cuts across the quad heading the other direction. Doug nudges you with his elbow. "Check it out." He says something about the one in the middle. Not crude exactly. Evaluative. Like she's a thing to assess. Keith picks one and gives her a number.

### Node — she glances back
The middle one glances back. She heard. Her expression doesn't change. She keeps walking.

**Micro-choices:**
- Look where they're looking. Shrug.
- Don't engage. Keep walking.
- "Come on, man."

### Node — walk continues (variant: absorbed)
The conversation moves on. Keith starts in on the football team's weekend. The building is up ahead.

### Node — walk continues (variant: deflected)
Doug and Keith keep going for another minute. You're a half-step behind them by the time you reach the building.

### Node — walk continues (variant: challenged)
Doug glances at you. Doesn't say anything. Keith does — short and confused. "What's your problem?" Then he answers his own question by changing the subject. The building is up ahead.

### Terminal choices
- Walk into the building together.
- Peel off at the entrance. You forgot something.

## Reading Notes

**On Jordan.** Jordan never speaks in this brief. He is established entirely through what he does not do — does not laugh at Peterson's joke, does not have a name on his door, does not have anyone in his room. The player who pays attention assembles him from negative space. The player who doesn't doesn't notice he's there.

**On the women.** Three women cross the quad in Beat 2C. One glances back. None of them are characters — they're scenery that occasionally looks back at you. They do not return. They do not get names. The game does not follow them home. This is the point.

**On Bryce.** Bryce in Beat 2E is the same Bryce from Anderson Hall and the party scenes. He's not malicious. He thinks he's being friendly. The "where are your parents from" question is asked with genuine curiosity. The brief preserves this — Bryce's flush in the challenged variant is embarrassment, not anger. He recovers fast.

**On the player's voice.** The challenged option is never articulate. It trails off, lands two words ("Come on, man"), corrects flatly without elaboration ("She said New Jersey"), or pushes back with the wrong vocabulary ("That joke's old, Peterson" — which is true, but isn't really what's wrong with it). The player knows something is off but does not have the 2023 vocabulary to name it. The trailing off and the imprecision are load-bearing. Fixing them in copy-edit would erase the time-traveler experience.

**On accumulation.** Several nodes carry text variants gated on prior choices — Beat 2B's settle-in greeting changes if the player challenged in Beat 2A; Beat 2G's hallway observation deepens if the player has challenged twice or more. These are not branches. They are the same scene, lit slightly differently by what the player has done before. The player will rarely notice the variant on a single playthrough. The variants are there for the second playthrough, and for the player whose pattern accumulates enough to make the lighting shift palpable.
