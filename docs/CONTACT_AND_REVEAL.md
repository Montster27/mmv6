## The Reveal Song: "Gangsta's Paradise" (Coolio, 1995)

The contact hums or sings a few bars of Gangsta's Paradise while crossing the quad. In 1983, this song won't exist for another 12 years. It's unmistakable — the Stevie Wonder sample gives it a haunting, almost hymnal quality that carries even when hummed. A player character from the future would recognize it instantly. A normal 1983 student wouldn't.

**Why it works:**
- Melodically distinctive — the Stevie Wonder "Pastime Paradise" hook is recognizable even hummed
- Not a novelty pick — it carries weight and seriousness
- 1995 release date puts it firmly outside the 1983 window
- It's culturally ubiquitous enough that the player character would know it
- The tone (brooding, reflective, slightly ominous) fits the contact's personality

**Important:** The song is never named in the scene. The player character recognizes the melody. The dialogue is something like "Hey — is that...?" and the contact's reaction confirms it. We don't spell out what the song is. Players who know it will feel the recognition. Players who don't will still get that the melody is wrong for 1983.

## The Contact Character

### Appearance & Demeanor
An upperclassman. Looks wise — something in the eyes that suggests they've been here longer than their transcript says. But cagey. Always looking around. Not paranoid exactly, but aware of who's nearby, who might overhear.

They're circumspect. They measure their words. They don't give you a manifesto — they give you a nudge and a direction. Everything they say has the quality of someone who knows far more than they're telling you, and who has reasons for the restraint.

**Not defined yet:** Name, gender, race, specific year at Harwick. These should be decided during content writing, when the voice emerges naturally. What matters now is the behavioral profile.

### What They Tell You

The contact doesn't give you a mission briefing. They give you practical advice that doubles as strategic direction:

**1. Build a strong social network.**
You'll need people. Not just friends — allies, contacts, people in different circles. The lone wolf doesn't make it.

**2. Make money. Have financial power.**
Resources matter. You need independence. Understand business, how money moves, how to generate it.

**3. Gain knowledge. Be in the right places.**
This is where the academic paths open up. The contact hints that different kinds of knowledge serve different purposes:
- **History** — know what happened so you understand what created the adversaries in this world. Who has power and why.
- **Physics** — understand the mechanics of what's happening to you. Time travel has rules even if you don't know them yet.
- **Computer Science** — the future runs on this. Getting ahead of the curve matters.
- **Sociology / Political Science** — understand how groups work, how power structures form, how to influence them.
- **Business** — practical path to financial independence and resource generation.

These aren't presented as a menu. They emerge from the conversation naturally — the contact mentions them in passing, as things that "turned out to matter."

**4. Work together.**
There are others. The goal is to make the world better. Not through force — through positioning, knowledge, and cooperation.

### What They Don't Tell You

- How time travel works
- How many others there are
- Who the adversaries are specifically
- What the endgame looks like
- Why this is happening

They say: as time goes on, things will trigger more memories. A better understanding. It can't be rushed. You just have to live it.

### The Caveat: History Is Not Exact

The contact delivers one critical warning, either directly or through implication:

**This is not exactly the same history you remember.**

There will be anomalies. Shifts. Things that are different from what you expect. You cannot use your future knowledge as a reliable playbook. The broad strokes may be similar, but the details diverge.

**The sports rule:** This is the most concrete example. Almost none of the games go as expected. If you remember the '83 World Series, don't bet on it. The outcomes are scrambled. This serves three design purposes:
1. It prevents the player from feeling like a prophet (which would undermine the life-sim)
2. It creates genuine uncertainty even for a character with future knowledge
3. It makes the "knowledge" academic paths genuinely valuable — you can't coast on memory alone

This rule extends beyond sports to politics, economics, cultural events, and personal encounters. The world rhymes with the one you remember, but it doesn't repeat.

### Reappearance Rules

The contact appears once in the opening (the quad scene). They may appear once more in Arc One — briefly, in passing, to check in or deliver one more nudge. They are not a quest-giver or a recurring mentor. They're a catalyst who steps back.

In later arcs, the contact may become more significant. For now, they're a door that opens and then closes.

## How This Opens Academic Paths

The contact's hints plant seeds for the player's academic choices, which won't become mechanically relevant until Week 2+ when class selection and study focus enter the game. But the seeds are planted here:

| Academic Path | What It Serves | Contact's Hint |
|---|---|---|
| History | Understanding adversaries, power structures | "Know what happened. Know who built this." |
| Physics | Understanding time travel mechanics | "The rules exist. You just don't know them yet." |
| Computer Science | Future-proofing, technical advantage | "There's a machine revolution coming. Be ready." |
| Sociology / Poli Sci | Social influence, group dynamics | "Learn how groups think. That's where the leverage is." |
| Business | Money generation, resource independence | "Money is freedom. Don't let anyone else hold your leash." |

The player doesn't choose a path in this scene. The paths are laid out as possibilities that will emerge through later storylet choices (which study group to join, which professor to pursue, which elective to take, etc.).

## The Anomaly Rule (Design Principle)

**"The past rhymes but doesn't repeat."**

This is a core world-building rule that affects every system:

- **Sports outcomes** are scrambled. Never match real 1983 results.
- **Political events** follow similar trajectories but with different timing or actors.
- **Cultural moments** may happen earlier, later, or not at all.
- **Personal encounters** are unique to this timeline — you can't predict who you'll meet or how they'll react based on "last time."

This means:
- Future knowledge gives you **frameworks** (understanding trends, technologies, social patterns) but not **specifics** (who wins, what stock goes up, when things happen)
- Academic knowledge is more valuable than memory — you need to understand *why* things happen, not just *what* happened
- The player can never feel omniscient, which preserves the scarcity-and-choice tension of the core game

This should be documented in CLAUDE.md and the content-creator agent docs as a writing rule: **never let the player character use specific future knowledge to predict specific outcomes.**
