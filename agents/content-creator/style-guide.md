# MMV Style Guide

## Core Philosophy

Every word must earn its place. The prose should read like compressed literary fiction — Rohinton Mistry's specificity, Raymond Carver's subtext, Denis Johnson's sensory commitment, Lorrie Moore's wry interiority. The reader fills in the emotional gap. The prose provides the physical detail that makes that gap visible.

## Reference Points

### Literary
- **Rohinton Mistry** — specificity over atmosphere. Every sentence is precisely calibrated. No filler, no decoration. Detail does double duty (physical fact + emotional resonance).
- **Raymond Carver** — subtext carries the weight. Characters say one thing and mean another. The reader infers.
- **Denis Johnson** — concrete sensory detail over emotional narration. "The fluorescent light buzzed and one tube was dead" > "the room felt depressing."
- **Richard Russo** — ordinary people making small decisions that accumulate into who they become. Funny, self-aware, still making mistakes.
- **Lorrie Moore** — interiority that's wry, not melodramatic. Characters observe themselves with half a smile.
- **Alice Munro** — time jumps, gaps as content, the reader assembles meaning from what's shown and what's withheld.

### Game Writing
- **Disco Elysium** — internal voice can be unreliable, funny, contradictory, and poetic simultaneously. Skill checks are voices with character.
- **Kentucky Route Zero** — prose as atmosphere. Compressed, imagistic, full of negative space. Dialogue often doesn't explain itself.
- **80 Days (Inkle / Meg Jayanth)** — economy of short passages. Every passage has one grounding sensory detail, one character beat, one forward pull. Never all three in the same sentence.

### What to Avoid
- **Bioware/RPG style** — competent but flat. Prose as furniture. Characters say what they mean.
- **Naughty Dog cinematics** — emotional manipulation through orchestrated spectacle.
- **Bethesda lore dumps** — worldbuilding mistaken for storytelling.
- **Indie game melancholy** — everything bittersweet and slightly precious.

## Sentence-Level Rules

### Specificity Over Atmosphere
Don't write "the dining hall was noisy and overwhelming." Write "someone dropped a tray near the salad bar and three people looked up, then looked away." The first is a summary. The second is a moment you're inside.

### Concrete Nouns Carry the Era
The 1983 texture arrives through objects, not exposition. A character doesn't "check the time" — they glance at a Casio digital watch. Someone isn't "listening to music" — the tinny bleed from their Walkman headphones is playing something you almost recognize. The period lives in the nouns.

### Verbs Do the Emotional Work
Mistry rarely uses adjectives for mood. The way a character *does* something tells you how they feel. Someone doesn't "nervously approach" — they "set their tray down two seats away and pretended to read the back of a cereal box." The verb and the action contain the emotion.

### Vary Sentence Rhythm
Short sentences for impact. Long sentences for immersion. A short sentence after a longer one creates emphasis. Three short sentences in a row creates urgency. A long sentence that accumulates detail creates the feeling of being inside a moment. AI defaults to medium-length sentences of roughly equal weight — the written equivalent of a monotone. Break that pattern.

### Trust the Gap
What you don't say matters more than what you do. If a character is lonely, don't mention loneliness. Show them eating alone and noticing the group at the next table laughing at something they can't hear. The reader's brain fills in "lonely" and it hits harder because they arrived at it themselves.

## Choice Label Rules

Choice labels are the most compressed writing in the game. They should work like the first line of a poem — evocative, physical, slightly ambiguous.

### Good Labels Put You in Your Body
- Bad: "Talk to the guy sitting alone"
- Better: "Take the open seat across from him"
- Best: "Slide your tray over before you can talk yourself out of it"

### Good Labels Imply Without Naming
- Bad: "Avoid social interaction"
- Better: "Keep your head down"
- Best: "Bus your tray and slip out the side door"

### Good Labels Have Forward Momentum
The label should feel like the first beat of an action, not a description of a strategy. The player is committing to a physical movement, not selecting from a menu.

---

## Outcome/Reaction Text Rules

After a choice resolves, show what happened. Don't evaluate it.

**Bad:**
> "You feel a sense of relief as you sit down and introduce yourself. Maybe this won't be so hard after all."

**Good:**
> "He looks up. 'Oh hey.' He moves his backpack off the chair. 'I was starting to think this whole table was quarantined or something.'"

The first tells you what to feel and wraps it up neatly. The second gives you a person reacting, lets you feel the relief yourself, and leaves the moment open.

## The Anti-Pattern Blacklist

The following constructions must never appear in MMV prose. If the editorial pass finds any of these, it must flag and revise.

### Emotional Narration Shortcuts
- "A mix of [emotion] and [emotion]"
- "Something about [noun] made you feel..."
- "You couldn't help but..."
- "It was clear that..."
- "For the first time, you..."
- "Despite yourself, you..."
- "The weight of [abstraction]"
- "You realized that..."
- "There was something [adjective] about the way..."
- "In that moment..."
- "A wave of [emotion] washed over..."

### Structural AI-isms
- "Part of you wanted X. But another part..." (symmetrical interiority)
- Starting paragraphs with "As..." followed by a gerund
- Paired semicolons in dialogue attribution
- Paragraphs that end with an emotional summary sentence
- Symmetrical constructions: "slightly more X and slightly less Y"
- Three-beat lists where each item escalates neatly

### Vocabulary Flags
- "Genuinely" / "honestly" / "straightforward"
- "Traverse" / "navigate" / "negotiate" (when "walk" / "find" / "deal with" will do)
- "Couldn't quite put your finger on" — cliché disguised as thought
- "Something shifted" — vague emotional transition
- "The silence spoke volumes" — dead metaphor
- Any construction using "palpable"

### Voice Violations
- Characters who are perfectly articulate about their feelings
- The narrator explaining what a choice means
- Tidy emotional resolution at the end of a beat
- Generic sensory details ("the warm glow of..." — which warm glow? Be specific)
- Metaphors too polished for an 18-year-old's interiority

## Period-Specific Writing Rules

- Characters don't "process" emotions — that's therapy language not common in 1983
- Nobody "validates" anyone's feelings
- No one "sets boundaries" as a conscious act (they do it, they just don't name it)
- People are less verbally articulate about inner states — show through action
- Profanity is more casual and less self-conscious than contemporary usage
- Humor is drier, less self-referential, less meta
- Physical descriptions should include what people are wearing — clothing was a much stronger identity signal
- Reference points are shared cultural objects (a movie everyone saw, an album everyone heard), not niche taste

## Voice Rules for MMV

The narrator isn't omniscient and isn't neutral. The narration has slight second-person interiority — it knows what the player is feeling but expresses it through observation and physical sensation, not direct emotional statement.

Closer to: "your hands are cold and you can't remember if you packed gloves"
Not: "you feel anxious about the weather"

The narrator has a slight Mistry-like quality: attentive to small absurdities, respectful of people's dignity, never mocking, but willing to notice when something is a little ridiculous. A kid carrying a campus map upside down. The RA reading the fire safety protocol like she's being held hostage.

## Revision Patterns

When the editorial pass flags a problem, use these revision strategies:

### "Naming the feeling" → Cut or replace with action
- Before: "You feel a pang of homesickness"
- After: "The sheets from home still smell like your mother's detergent. You notice it, then you don't."

### "Symmetrical construction" → Break symmetry, stay concrete
- Before: "Each thing you place makes the room slightly more yours and slightly less his"
- After: "You put your alarm clock where his alarm clock already isn't. The room adjusts."

### "Evaluative one-liner ending" → Let the last detail be the ending
- Before: "You know who you'd eat with if the dining hall felt too big. That's not nothing."
- After: "You know who you'd eat with if the dining hall felt too big." (stop there)

### "Metaphor too polished for character" → Ground in concrete prediction
- Before: "Not friendship exactly — but a frequency. You're on the same one."
- After: "Not friendship exactly. But you'd eat lunch with this person tomorrow without having to invent a reason."

### "Labeling the déjà vu" → Render it without naming it
- Before: "The déjà vu is faint this time, more like a shadow than a flash"
- After: "The feeling is there and then it isn't, like catching someone's eye across a room and finding they were looking past you."
