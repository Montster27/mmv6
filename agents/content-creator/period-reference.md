# MMV Period Reference: 1983

## Demographic Reality

### Campus Composition
A midsized Midwestern university (8,000-12,000 students) in 1983:
- ~86-90% white (higher than national average for Midwest non-flagship)
- ~4-7% Black
- ~2-3% Hispanic/Latino
- ~1-2% Asian-American
- Tiny percentage international students

This is not a design choice. It's demographic fact. The campus is overwhelmingly white, and non-white students navigate that reality in every interaction.

### What This Means for Writing

**Non-white characters have full interiority.** They have the same density of personal concerns as anyone else, with the additional layer of navigating a white-majority space. They are not defined by their race — they are defined by their choices, fears, ambitions, and relationships, with race as one of many factors shaping their experience.

**White characters have ethnicity.** "White" is not a monolith in the Midwest in 1983. Danny from Parma, Ohio has Polish heritage. That affects what food feels like home, how his family talks, their attitude toward college. Irish, Italian, German, WASP — these are legible ethnic identities on campus. Use them.

**Microaggressions happen but aren't labeled.** The word didn't exist in 1983. The experiences did. Someone assumes Miguel is on a sports scholarship. A professor is surprised by a Black student's writing quality. Someone asks "where are you really from?" Write these as things that happen — not as lessons, not with narrative commentary. The character experiences it. The reader understands it. The prose doesn't explain.

**Social geography is physical.** The dining hall has de facto seating patterns. Black students often know each other and sit together — not because of rules but because they're 5% of campus and have already had the same conversation with forty white strangers today. Render this through what the room looks like. Don't narratorialize it.

**Economic and racial intersections are concrete.** The bookstore scene — watching someone put a book back — is where class and race can intersect without the text announcing it.

## Sexuality and the 1983 Campus

### The Social Climate
- Most gay students are closeted. This is survival, not a character flaw.
- On a midsized Midwestern campus, being openly gay meant social isolation at best, physical danger at worst.
- "That's so gay" was a reflexive insult. "Fag" was thrown around in dorm hallways. This language should appear naturally — it's what 1983 sounded like.
- AIDS was known but not yet personally experienced on most campuses. It was news, rumor, a Newsweek cover. For gay students, it was terrifying and isolating. For most straight students, it was abstract.
- Gay student organizations existed but were small, sometimes targeted, and often met with institutional indifference or hostility.
- Therapy culture was not mainstream. There were no campus "safe spaces" (the concept didn't exist in that form). No "ally" language. "Coming out" existed as a phrase but was much heavier than its contemporary usage.

### Writing Rules for Sexuality
- If a character is gay or questioning, render it through what they notice and don't say, reactions they suppress, the gap between public performance and private experience.
- Homophobia should come from otherwise decent people — casual, reflexive, unexamined. Not cartoonish villainy.
- The player character's sexuality is not predetermined. Choices that have queer potential should be available without labels — a moment of noticing someone's hands, a conversation that goes a beat longer than expected.
- Never use contemporary language: no "coming out" as casual, no "ally," no "safe space," no "microaggression," no "validate."
- Never write "very special episodes" about sexuality. It's a thread in a life, not a lesson.

## Physical Objects of 1983

Use these in prose. They ground scenes in the period through specificity.

### In the Dorm Room
- Cassette player / boom box (not CD player — those arrive 1984-85)
- Walkman (Sony, the status version) or knockoff
- Typewriter (electric or manual) — some students have early word processors
- Rotary or touch-tone phone in the hallway (not in rooms)
- Whiteboard or corkboard on room door
- Posters attached with thumbtacks or masking tape (not Command strips)
- Alarm clock (analog or early digital with red LED numbers)
- Fan (no AC in most 70s-era dorm buildings)
- Hot pot for ramen (technically against the rules)

### On Campus
- Bulletin boards (layers of flyers, thumbtacked, curling)
- Paper class schedules folded in back pockets
- Carbon-copy sign-up sheets at club tables
- Card catalogs in the library (not computerized)
- Microfiche machines for newspapers
- Arcade cabinets in the student union
- Vending machines (Coke, Tab, Dr Pepper — cans with pull-tabs)
- Pay phones (rotary dial or push-button, collect calls common)
- Campus newspaper printed on broadsheet

### Money and Commerce
- Checks (personal checking accounts, balancing a checkbook)
- Cash (bills and coins, no debit cards)
- No ATMs on most campuses (they existed but weren't ubiquitous)
- Textbooks priced $15-45 each (Biology text at $45 is expensive)
- Pizza slice: $2-3. Coffee at a diner: $0.50. Movie ticket: $3.
- Minimum wage: $3.35/hour (raised to this in 1981)
- Semester tuition at a school like Harwick: roughly $2,000-4,000

### Clothing (Identity Signals)
- Preppy: polo shirts, khakis, penny loafers, sweaters tied over shoulders
- New Wave: skinny ties, angular haircuts, thrift store blazers
- Jock: letterman jacket, sneakers (Nikes, Converse), mesh shorts
- Punk (rare at a Midwestern school): ripped jeans, band tees, combat boots
- Default male freshman: jeans, sneakers, concert t-shirt or button-down
- Default female freshman: jeans, Keds or flats, oversized sweater or blouse
- Members Only jackets (aspirational)
- Jordache, Levi's, Lee jeans

## Slang and Language

### Use Lightly (Salt, Don't Saturate)
- "Awesome," "rad," "totally," "bummer," "bogus"
- "No way!" / "For sure" / "Chill out"
- "Dude" (becoming gender-neutral)
- "Like..." (increasing Valley Girl influence from the coasts)

### Subculture Labels (Used by Students)
- "Preppy" (mainstream campus aesthetic)
- "Punk" / "New Waver"
- "Yuppie" (young urban professional — used cynically)
- "Sellout" / "Square"

### Language That Did NOT Exist or Was Not Common
- "Awesome" was fine. "Amazing" as a general intensifier was not yet standard.
- "Toxic" (as a personality descriptor)
- "Triggered" / "trauma" (in casual usage)
- "Boundaries" (as a relationship concept in everyday speech)
- "Ghosting" / "gaslighting" / "love bombing"
- "Self-care"
- "Reach out" (meaning to contact someone — this was corporate jargon)
- "Take space" / "hold space"
- "Unpack" (meaning to analyze)
- "Vibe" (as a noun used this way)
- "Low-key" / "high-key"
- "Literally" (as an intensifier)

## Music as Identity

Music mattered as social currency. Your record collection told people who you were.

### What's Actually Playing on Campus (Fall 1983)
- The Police — *Synchronicity* (huge, unavoidable)
- Michael Jackson — *Thriller* (still everywhere from 1982)
- Talking Heads — *Speaking in Tongues*
- R.E.M. — *Murmur* (college radio, the "cool" pick)
- U2 — *War* (rising)
- The Clash — *Combat Rock* (past peak but still credible)
- Duran Duran — *Rio* (pop, MTV-driven)
- Billy Joel — *An Innocent Man*
- Van Halen — *1984* (coming January, but anticipation)

### How to Use Music in Prose
- Name specific songs or albums when a character is playing something. "Someone is playing a song" is vague. "The tinny opening of 'Every Breath You Take' from two doors down" is specific.
- Music signals identity. The kid playing R.E.M. is different from the kid playing Van Halen. Neither is wrong. Both are saying something.
- Walkman headphones bleed sound. You hear other people's music whether you want to or not. This is a texture detail.

## News and Background Anxiety

### What's in the Air (September 1983)
- Cold War tension is high. Reagan's "Evil Empire" speech was March 1983.
- The Korean Air Lines Flight 007 shootdown happened September 1, 1983 — this is literally the month the game starts. It's on the news. Students are talking about it.
- Nuclear freeze movement is active on campuses.
- AIDS is in the news but poorly understood. Mostly associated with gay men in New York and San Francisco. On a Midwestern campus, it's abstract but present.
- The economy is recovering from the early-80s recession. Unemployment is still high but dropping. "Getting a good job" is a real anxiety.
- MTV is two years old and reshaping how young people consume culture.

### How to Use This
- News appears as dorm TV conversations, newspaper headlines on the common room table, someone's offhand comment at dinner.
- Nuclear anxiety occasionally intrudes — not as plot, but as background dread. The same way climate anxiety works today.
- Economic anxiety is personal: "will this degree actually get me a job?"
- Don't lecture. Let the world be present. A headline on a folded newspaper. A TV playing in an empty common room.

## The Emotional Climate of 1983

- Less emotional language available. People didn't have the vocabulary for feelings that exists today.
- Therapy was not normalized. Suggesting someone "talk to someone" was a bigger deal.
- Gender roles were less flexible but actively being questioned.
- Parental relationships were more formal. Less emotional dialogue between generations.
- Physical risk was more normalized. Seatbelt laws were just being enacted. Drinking age was being raised to 21 (1984 federal law). Campuses were less supervised.
- Privacy was physical, not digital. If you wanted to be alone, you closed a door. If you wanted to know what someone was doing, you had to be there.
